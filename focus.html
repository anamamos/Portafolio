Using sound and AI to help students concentrate and stay well in digital learning environments.

Role
Research, analysis, concept & design strategy
Context
Academic research project
Approach

Introduction

Sound, designed on purpose.

Digital learning has expanded access to education — but it has also introduced new challenges around attention, focus and cognitive overload. In virtual contexts students are constantly exposed to distractions and often resort to multitasking, which hurts learning outcomes.

This project explores how sound — specifically background music — can be intentionally designed to support concentration and well-being during academic tasks, and how AI can enable more adaptive, effective learning experiences.

The problem

University students struggle to stay focused online.

Focus in digital learning is fragile, and the ways students try to protect it are largely improvised and unmeasured.

  • 01Many students listen to music while studying, but that music isn't designed to support cognitive processes.
  • 02Educators face limitations integrating music into content because of copyright restrictions.
  • 03Concentration is usually evaluated subjectively, without objective data to back design decisions.
The opportunity

What if sound became a designed tool, not a personal guess?

This project reframes sound as an active component of the learning experience — one that can be shaped through research, data and technology.

Instead of leaving background audio to chance, it treats sound as something that can be designed, measured and adapted to the learner and the task.

Research question
The question that guided everything

How can sound be strategically designed to improve concentration and well-being in digital learning environments?

Research approach

A mixed-method study, built for evidence.

I combined qualitative and quantitative methods to understand how different kinds of sound actually change attention — not just how they feel.

Surveys→Interviews→Eye tracking→Data analysis
  • 01Surveys with university students
  • 02Exploratory interviews
  • 03Controlled experiments using eye tracking
  • 04Comparison across three sound conditions: silence, conventional music, and AI-generated music (NEA)
Measuring focus

Attention, measured objectively.

Focus was measured with eye tracking, a well-established method in cognitive and attention research. Indicators analysed included fixation duration, number of fixations, time to first fixation, response time and task performance.

Participants completed three academic task types — critical reading, quantitative reasoning and visuospatial processing — each performed under the three sound conditions, enabling a direct comparison of attention patterns and performance.

  • 01Key insight: more visual attention does not necessarily mean better concentration.
Key findings

What the data actually showed.

  • 01Background music alters attentional patterns, even without conscious awareness.
  • 02AI-generated music produced more efficient attention patterns, associated with better performance.
  • 03More fixations don't equal better focus — they often signal higher cognitive effort.
  • 04Silence isn't always the optimal condition for complex learning tasks.
  • 05The quality of cognitive processing matters more than the quantity of visual attention.
The solution

An adaptive sound platform for digital education.

The proposed solution lets educators integrate AI-generated background music directly into educational content — so sound becomes a functional design element, not a decorative layer. The flow: educator → platform → AI sound engine → student.

01

Copyright-free generation

AI-generated audio removes the licensing barriers that stop educators using sound in the first place.

02

Adapts to context

Sound tuned to the task type, the audience and the learning moment — not one track for everything.

03

Reduced cognitive friction

Audio designed to support processing rather than compete with it.

04

Focus without distraction

A richer learning experience that keeps attention where it belongs.

Research & data appendix

Quantitative methodology.

For readers interested in the research foundations behind the design decisions: attention was measured objectively with eye tracking; variables included total and average fixation duration, number of fixations, time to first fixation and pupil diameter. Statistical analysis was conducted using ANOVA.

Key result

AI ≠ noise

Statistically significant differences between sound conditions

AI-generated music was associated with more efficient attention patterns across tasks.

Key result

Silence ≠ best

Longer fixation times, no better performance

Silence produced longer fixation times in some tasks without improving performance — quiet isn't automatically optimal.

Personal learnings

Design as a tool for cognition.

This project reinforced my interest in designing experiences that go beyond visual interfaces — experiences that intentionally influence cognitive states using real data and research.

It reframed how I think about design: as a way to modulate attention, cognition and well-being, not just to arrange screens.

Future vision

Learning environments that adapt to how we think.

This research opens the door to personalised educational systems where sound dynamically adapts to a learner's cognitive needs — supporting focus, motivation and long-term learning outcomes, with design decisions informed by objective data and human cognitive needs.
